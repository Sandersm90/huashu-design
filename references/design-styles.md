## I. Information-architecture school (01–04)
> Philosophy: **data is not decoration; it is building material**.

### 01. Pentagram / Michael Bierut
**Philosophy**: typography is language; the grid is thought made visible.

**Traits**:
- extremely restrained color, often black/white plus one brand accent
- a contemporary Swiss grid sensibility
- typography as the main visual voice
- strategic negative space, often **60%+ whitespace**

### 02. Stamen Design
**Philosophy**: make data feel like a landscape you can almost touch.

**Traits**:
- cartographic thinking applied to information design
- algorithmic forms with an organic feel
- warm data-viz palettes: terracotta, sage, deep blue
- layered information systems with a tactile, crafted tone

### 03. Information Architects
**Philosophy**: design is not decoration; it is the architecture of content.

**Traits**:
- radical hierarchy clarity
- system fonts as a reading-first choice, not a shortcut
- loyalty to classic blue hyperlinks
- performance as an aesthetic value

### 04. Fathom
**Philosophy**: every pixel must carry information.

**Traits**:
- scientific-journal rigor with editorial elegance
- exact quantitative visualization
- calm, professional palettes: gray, navy, one highlight
- footnotes and citations treated as designed elements, not afterthoughts

> Philosophy: **technology itself can behave like flowing poetry**.

### 05. Locomotive
**Philosophy**: scrolling is not browsing; it is a journey.

### 06. Active Theory
**Philosophy**: making technology visible makes it more legible.

### 07. Field.io
**Philosophy**: code is not just a tool; it becomes the designer.

### 08. Resn
**Philosophy**: every click should move the story forward.

> Philosophy: **reduction is not emptiness; it is judgment**.

### 09. Experimental Jetset
**Philosophy**: one idea, one form.

**Philosophy**: objectivity is a form of beauty.

### 11. Build
**Philosophy**: refined simplicity is harder than complexity.

## How to use the prompt DNA

Formula:
`[style DNA] + [scene template from scene-templates.en.md] + [specific content description]`

## Core prompt principle: describe mood, not layout

Short prompts with emotional clarity, material feel, and lineage usually work better than over-constrained layout instructions.

**Bad**:
- exact percentages for every color
- mechanically specifying every layout slot
- listing every object to include

**Good**:
- editorial mood
- known visual lineage
- what the viewer should feel
- what the dominant concept should be

## Example

**Bad**: “Dark background, centered title, left bullets, right chart, 60/30/10 color split...”

**Good**: “A Bloomberg Businessweek-like editorial spread where the number ‘28.5%’ dominates the page like a headline, in warm cream tones with sharp black typography.”

## Default aesthetic taboo list

Unless the brand explicitly calls for it:
- avoid default cyber-neon deep-blue palettes
- avoid stray signatures or watermark-like marks on cover images
- avoid generic ‘AI’ symbols standing in for actual taste
